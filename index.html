Mental Health in Australia

Who is most affected, and are services keeping up?

Mental health is a growing concern in Australia, affecting people across different ages, genders and locations. This visualisation explores how common mental disorders are, which groups are most affected, and whether Australia’s mental health services appear to be keeping up with demand.

The story begins with the overall scale of mental illness, then focuses on age groups most at risk, before looking at the availability of mental health practitioners and service contacts across Australia.

The Scale of Mental Illness

Before examining specific groups, it is important to understand the overall scale of mental illness in Australia. This section explores how common mental disorders are, which types are most prevalent, and whether prevalence differs between males and females.

How common is mental illness?

Around one in five Australians experienced a mental disorder within a 12-month period. Each square below represents 1% of the population.

Are males and females affected differently?

Mental health prevalence is not evenly distributed between genders. This chart compares the prevalence of specific disorders among males and females.

What types of disorders are most common?

Mental illness is not a single condition. These charts break mental disorders into major categories and show which specific disorders contribute most to Australia's mental health burden.

Key takeaway: Within each mental disorder category, a small number of disorder types make up most of the prevalence. Depressive episodes dominate affective disorders, social phobia is the largest anxiety disorder type, and alcohol-related disorders account for the biggest share of substance use disorders.

Age Groups Most Affected

Age is one of the clearest patterns in Australia's mental health data. This section examines which age groups experience the highest prevalence, how prevalence has changed over time, and whether increasing prevalence is reflected in growing demand for mental health services.

Which age group has the highest prevalence?

This chart ranks age groups by the proportion experiencing mental disorders.

Key takeaway: Young adults aged 16–24 report the highest prevalence of mental disorders among all age groups.

How has prevalence changed?

Has this always been the case? Comparing 2007 and 2022 reveals how mental health prevalence has changed across age groups over time.

Key takeaway: The largest increases occurred among younger age groups, suggesting mental health challenges have become increasingly concentrated among younger Australians.

Mental health score over time

Prevalence rates tell only part of the story. Mental health scores provide another measure of wellbeing across age groups and over time.

Service contacts by age group

This chart shows how community mental health contacts changed from 2015 to 2024.

Key takeaway: Younger Australians consistently record lower mental health scores, with noticeable declines during the COVID-19 period.

Are Services Keeping Up?

Rising demand creates pressure on mental health services. The final section examines whether Australia's mental health workforce has expanded quickly enough to support growing service demand.

Workforce growth

The first step is to examine whether the workforce itself has grown. This chart compares practitioner numbers in 2015 and 2024.

Key takeaway: All major professions expanded, with psychologists showing the largest workforce growth over the period.

Where are mental health practitioners located?

Workforce growth does not necessarily mean equal access. This map shows how practitioner availability varies across Australian states and territories.

Key findings

Practitioner availability is not evenly distributed across Australia.

Darker states have more mental health practitioners per 100,000 people, while lighter states have fewer practitioners relative to population size.

This suggests that access to mental health support may vary depending on where Australians live.

Practitioners vs service contacts

Ultimately, workforce capacity must be considered alongside demand. This chart compares practitioner numbers with mental health service contacts across Australia.

Key takeaway: Although states with larger workforces generally record more service contacts, demand continues to grow alongside workforce expansion.
